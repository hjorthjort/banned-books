# Tag Taxonomy

This file documents the current tag system for the Forbidden Books Wiki.

Two distinct tag families exist:

- `ban reason tags`: why a censor or state said the work should be restricted.
- `criticism target tags`: who or what the work is criticizing, mocking, exposing, or arguing against.

Guideline:

- Reason tags should reflect the censor's stated rationale, even when the rationale is abusive, vague, or dishonest.
- Criticism target tags should name the institution, religion, regime, or social order the work is aimed at.

## Ban Reason Tags

### Sexuality and Morality

| Tag | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `profanity` | Profane, vulgar, or insulting language cited as offensive. |
| `indecency` | Broad indecency claims not framed as a formal obscenity charge. |
| `obscenity` | The work was treated as obscene under law or official policy. |
| `sexual-explicitness` | Graphic or unusually candid sexual content. |
| `sexuality` | Sexual subject matter was cited more generally. |
| `queer-content` | Same-sex desire or gender nonconformity was targeted by censors. |
| `gender-politics` | Feminist or gender-order disputes triggered censorship. |
| `child-protection` | Officials claimed the work endangered children or youth readers. |
| `morality` | General moral corruption or immorality was cited. |
| `public-morality` | Officials framed the ban as necessary to protect public morals. |
| `moral-panic` | The ban was driven by a wider panic over corruption or social decay. |

### Religion and Belief

| Tag | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `blasphemy` | The work was said to insult a deity, prophet, or sacred matter. |
| `religious-offense` | Authorities said the work offended a religion or its believers. |
| `religious-criticism` | The work criticizes doctrine, authority, or sacred history. |
| `religious-content` | Religious subject matter itself triggered censorship. |
| `religious-control` | Authorities controlled access for sectarian or confessional reasons. |
| `religious-dissent` | The work challenged dominant religious authority or orthodoxy. |
| `religious-morality` | Censors invoked a specifically religious moral code. |
| `anti-clericalism` | The work attacked clergy, church institutions, or hierarchy. |
| `doctrinal-control` | Authorities intervened to police doctrine or theology. |
| `heresy` | The text was condemned as heretical or doctrinally deviant. |
| `secularism` | The work was targeted for defending or embodying secular values. |

### Politics and State

| Tag | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `political-dissent` | The work opposed or undermined the ruling order. |
| `political-sensitivity` | Officials treated the subject as too politically delicate to circulate. |
| `political-control` | The work was suppressed to preserve direct political control over discourse. |
| `political-danger` | Authorities described the work as politically dangerous or destabilizing. |
| `ideological-control` | The ban aimed to enforce an official ideology. |
| `anti-state` | The work was framed as hostile to the state or its institutions. |
| `anti-communism` | The work criticized communist doctrine or regimes. |
| `anti-dictatorship` | The work attacked dictatorship or authoritarian rule. |
| `anti-fascism` | The work opposed fascist rule or ideology. |
| `anti-imperialism` | The work attacked empire or foreign domination. |
| `anti-militarism` | The work challenged militarism or martial culture. |
| `anti-war` | The work opposed war or patriotic war culture. |
| `nazism` | Authorities cited Nazi ideology, Nazi propaganda, or explicit Nazi affiliation. |
| `decolonization` | The work was tied to anti-colonial or decolonizing politics. |
| `left-politics` | Authorities targeted socialist, radical, or left political content. |
| `revolutionary-politics` | The work was accused of promoting revolution or insurrection. |
| `utopian-politics` | The work's speculative political vision itself triggered alarm. |
| `sedition` | The work was treated as seditious or disloyal to lawful authority. |
| `national-security` | Authorities invoked security of the state or nation. |
| `state-secrecy` | The work revealed or was accused of revealing protected state information. |
| `public-order` | Officials said the work threatened unrest, riots, or disorder. |
| `wartime-censorship` | The work was restricted under wartime emergency controls. |
| `press-freedom` | The text's defense of press freedom or open printing triggered suppression. |
| `licensing` | The text was blocked for lacking required permission or approved publication status. |
| `unauthorized-history` | Authorities objected to an unapproved version of history or memory. |
| `offense-to-local-interests` | Officials said the work insulted local institutions, customs, or prestige. |
| `defamation` | The work was accused of defamatory attacks on persons, institutions, or the state. |

### Violence, Hate, and Harm

| Tag | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `violence` | Violent content itself was cited as objectionable. |
| `graphic-violence` | Extreme or graphic depictions of violence triggered the ban. |
| `incitement-to-violence` | The work was said to encourage violence or violent imitation. |
| `instructional-harm` | The text gives harmful instructions, such as for sabotage or self-destruction. |
| `self-harm` | The work was tied to suicide or other self-harm concerns. |
| `copycat-fear` | Officials feared readers would imitate what the work described. |
| `extremism` | Authorities treated the work as extremist propaganda or material. |
| `hate-speech` | The work was targeted for hatred toward a protected group. |
| `racism` | Racist content or racial insult was itself the basis for restriction. |
| `antisemitism` | Antisemitic ideology or propaganda was central to the ban rationale. |
| `discrimination` | Authorities cited discriminatory treatment of a group or class. |
| `ethnic-conflict` | The work was said to inflame ethnic hostility or communal tension. |
| `racial-politics` | Race conflict or racial critique was treated as politically dangerous. |

### Form and Special Content

| Tag | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `satire` | The work's satirical mode was itself treated as subversive or insulting. |
| `anthropomorphic-animals` | Authorities objected to animals acting or speaking like humans. |

## Notes

- `offensive language` is normalized to `profanity`.
- `queer sexuality` is normalized to `queer-content`.
- The `criticismTargets` field is seeded for obvious cases now and can be expanded gradually across the corpus.
